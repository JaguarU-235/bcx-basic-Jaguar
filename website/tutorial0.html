rB+ tutorials :: 0 for the absolute beginner

This text and the following tutorial documents are intended to help guide anyone interested in the possibilities of rB+. This first one will to give a brief, simplified and friendly picture of what the Jaguar is and how rB+ might allow you to get creative and productive with your console. Rather than tutorials covering how to program in BASIC, subsequent parts will cover more of the raptor-specific side of rB+, the general flow and organisation of a project, and a simple background to how it all comes together as software on your Jaguar or Virtual Jaguar.

DISCLAIMER: This isn't a technical document. Further episodes are likely to remain as equally devoid of technical facts and figures. It exists solely to help put people in the right frame of mind regarding the workings of the Jaguar, raptor and rB+. To achieve this, it over-simplifies quite liberally to keep things in a comfortably safe realm that parallels the very reason for rB+'s existence.

raptor BASIC plus - rB+ - is Reboot's most recent attempt to lower the bar for would-be developers, to break down yet more barriers preventing people becoming active on the open platform Jaguar. It is built on the technology that was created to form raptor. Throughout the years of Jaguar's homebrew infancy and all the way through to today, many people have contributed to the Jaguar cause by releasing their libraries, discovering tricks and tips and sharing their knowledge. Now things take a slightly different turn - this is finally it, thanks to all that came before, we're now at the point where anyone and everyone gets to give it a go... where creativity and flair are given the chance to one-up past efforts as a broader spectrum of designers, tinkerers and curious kids with a few spare hours finally get to participate without having to do masses of homework.

At least, that would be the case in an ideal world. As things stand there's a rather large barrier preventing much of this - documentation. That's something this site and these tutorials plan to address. What you are reading now is part zero. This is where we get things correctly aligned in our heads before we type a single line of code or define our first sprite.

raptorBAISC+ is a compiled BASIC, not an interpreted one, powered by BCX the BASIC to C translation tool. There's no IDE - just use notepad, n++ or which ever text editor you prefer. Edit a few text files on your PC, run a batch file and your BASIC code is translated to C which gets compiled, linked and hooks its claws into raptor, spitting out GPU-powered software you can share as BJL-style binaries or ROMs.

The text files are:

project.bas : your BASIC program
assets.txt  : where you tell rB+ which graphics, music and sounds to include
rapinit     : where you define the sprites based on those graphics
rapu235     : where you define the sound effects based on the samples

So that's rB+ - what actually is raptor?

raptor is CJ's hardware-abstracting package designed to take much of the grind out of game development on Jaguar, to keep developers busy with more of the fun aspects of making games rather than lost in technicalities and reinventing the wheel each time a new project is undertaken.

rB+ is the vehicle for placing this power into the hands of a broader user-base: raptor for all.

If you're new to Jaguar or have only a passing knowledge of the hardware specifics, this text will hopefully prove helpful in getting you thinking the "Jaguar way" and similarly the "raptor way" and remove any lingering fears after downloading the package and being presented with a whole load of files and only the fairly technical raptor manual for company (designed for assembler coders, not BASIC programmers). This is where we get past all that and break down any misconceptions we might have and build a clear and simple picture of the framework we'll work within.

Hopefully we're assuming nothing here. You may be familiar with creating games on other platforms, in BASIC or more low-level languages. OTOH, you could just be an avid Jaguar fan who has never even considered how to make a game but have spied the potential opportunity to dip a toe into Jaguar programming, something you might never have expected. Wherever you sit along that line, hopefully you'll shortly feel confident enough to give it a go. A few minutes from now you should be able to begin thinking how rB+ could allow you to explore your creative ideas and realise them as software running on the machine.

So what we're going to concentrate on first is the way the Jaguar builds the display we see on our TVs. This is fundamental and something we need to get right before going any further. We often read about how powerful the Jaguar is for 2D games, but do we know why this is so? It constructs the display we see with the use of its powerful 64-bit Object Processor (OP), but this processor is not one we have to directly program, it basically just digests a listed description of what we want to see on screen and constructs it. You could think of it as reading blueprint of what we want to see, seeing a list of the building blocks and where to find them, and then going off to build it for us. The blueprint is the Object List (which we'll mostly be referring to as the sprite list); the building blocks are objects (similarly, we'll be referring to these as sprites), and the display on screen is nothing more than a collage of these sprites. But oddly, that collage is not something that actually ever exists as a whole...

Instead of having dedicated video memory, somewhere we copy all these sprites to in order to create the whole scene, Jaguar produces the display a single line at a time, up to 50 or 60 times a second for every row of pixels that form our view on screen. Working this way, Jaguar only ever has to think about just those sprites involved in building a single row of pixels, line by line. That's worth giving some thought, so maybe it's a good time to go make a hot, strong cup of something and consider what implications that might have. Get the cogs turning the right way.

If you went for that drink, hopefully you're already thinking of some of the advantages to this method of managing the display and maybe even a potential problem or two that might arise. If you've realised that a single block of graphics data in memory can be used to describe any number of objects or sprites on screen without using any more memory, good work. That's obviously something rB+ lets you take full advantage of. You could have a single sprite or 50 sprites over an empty background, all based on the same block of graphics in RAM, both effectively being equal in terms of memory usage - just one sprite's worth. If you're worriedly considering how the OP might handle all 50 of these sprites on one line at a time, your concern is noted. However, Jaguar is powerful and you'll come to get a feel for what is and what is not possible as your experience grows. Half the fun is finding out the limits of what you can get away with and tricks to get around the problems.

Jaguar's sprite handling allows for a little code and some simple list scripting to be translated into often quite complex scenes and movements on screen, that's mostly thanks to the OP. raptor has it's own lists that align with these OP lists and manages them in the background. rB+ allows simple but powerful manipulation of the raptor lists that describe the sprites we see. There's nothing particularly difficult about setting up the lists, in fact it's just like filling in a form. Each list is made up of a series of standard sprite definitions. These definitions are nothing more than a series of properties that combine to describe a sprite's look and movements - such as number of colours, size, position on screen, and also automated properties that tick over in the background without programmer intervention such as animation and drift. There are also standard Jaguar features such as scaling, horizontal flipping and windowing on larger bitmaps, made possible just by defining or re-defining a sprite's properties. It's simple but powerful stuff.

Now we're getting a feel for how the Jaguar does its 2D magic, it's maybe time to consider at a single sprite definition and examine the majority of the properties - it should help give more of a flavour of the possibilities and further demystify things if we take a look now. A raptor list is nothing more than a series of these sprite definitions with a few markers here and there to allow raptor to manage them appropriately. The most potentially troublesome thing, and I believe this is why some people instantly baulked at the idea of rB+ as "requires assembler knowledge" (which is absolutely, categorically untrue) is that the list itself is contained in a hideously terrifying .S file.

An .S file!

Oh no! That must mean it's difficult, right? Well... in all fairness, it can and does look a little daunting the first time you come face to face with it. But there is really nothing to fear, it's just a blank form that is hungry for our numbers. Let's take a look at a slightly friendlier English version of a sprite definition. Everything here is directly comparable to an actual sprite definition.

Simplified sprite definition:

Sprite property Sprite property value Description
How many sprites like this one?
1 You can ask raptor to make 1 or more of the sprite, to save defining say 10 of the same enemy ship for example.
Is the sprite active? yes Yes means it will display when the list is processed by the OP, no means... you guessed it
Where will it display in x? 150 Distance from left side of screen for sprite to sit. This is the top left corner of the sprite.
Where will it display in y?
50 Distance down from the top of the screen for the sprite to sit. Also the top left corner of the sprite
Horizontal drift 1
 The sprite will move OVER one pixel from the left to the right every time the list is updated, automatically.
Vertical drift -2 The sprite will move UP two pixels from the bottom to the top every time the list is updated, automatically.
Sprite width 64 The sprite is 64 pixels wide
Sprite height 32 The sprite is 32 pixels tall
Flip sprite? No The sprite is not mirrored by the Jaguar hardware, so displays regularly.
Collision box details  width,height,distance from centre of sprite These 4 values are used for collision detection - so you can set up a sprite that is able to be checked against others to see if they collided - for instance an enemy and your bullets or your bullet and many enemies or many bullets vs many enemies.
Location BMP_CAR_SPRITE Just a nickname used to tell raptor where the graphics for this sprite sit in memory - you create this label nickname in another file where all the graphics, music and other extras are listed.
Graphics depth 16-bits Can be anything supported from 1-bit per pixel to 24-bits per pixel.
Graphics type  RGB This tells raptor that the graphics in RAM are regular Red/Green/Blue.
Transparent? Yes  Do you want the background colour of the sprite to paint over the sprites beneath it or do you want that colour to be transparent and let the other colour beneath show through?
2x Sprite size in memory stuff
These two are not pixel sizes, but the memory footprint they have. Basically dimensions x bit-depth
Animation speed 0 An animation speed of 0 will update the sprite animation every time the list is updated. 1 would wait until after the Jaguar is building the next screen, 2 would wait for it to do this twice and so on.
Number of additional frames of animation 7 This sprite has a total of 8 frames of animation, the 1 we described and 7 more like it following on.
Repeat the animation? Yes This sprite will animate fully and the loop back to the start and begin again.
Edge wrap? Yes If the sprite wanders off the edges of the screen, it will come back on at the other side.
How long do you want it active? infinite This sprite won't fade away after a given time, it'll stay awake until we tell it otherwise.
Track a path? No This sprite won't use a special tracking file to describe how it moves
If you want it to track, where is it? n/a  If we had it tracking, we could tell raptor where we keep the tracking path in RAM
scale sprite? Yes We've told raptor we want this sprite to be able to be scaled
x-scale? original We do not want to scale it horizontally
y-scale? 3x We want the sprite scaled vertically x3 - so it'll be 96 pixels high (32*3)
Was it hit? No There's a flag here that raptor can set to tell us something hit it if we're using collision. We're setting it to not be flagged at start.
Colour table? N/A For 1, 2, 4 and 8-bit graphics, we can specify which table of colours this sprite uses. Our sprite is 16-bit and therefore does not need a colour table, every pixel is described directly by the image, rather than as an index to a list of colour values.
Can this one be hit? No This sprite is not going to be used for collision checking.
What will raptor to do if it gets hit?  Nothing  You could tell raptor to remove the sprite if it gets hit.
Special collision box? Normal You can tell raptor to go look for a description of a special collision box description for non-boxy shapes.
How many hits?  N/A This tells raptor how many times this sprite can be hit before it is removed
How hard does it hit? N/A This tells raptor how hard this sprites hurts another when colliding.
Special width info N/A This helps us handle sprites that are windows into a larger graphic areas.

And that's pretty much all there is to it. A raptor list essentially just consists of a number of entries like the one above. Most entries in there can be directly tinkered with from within rB+ once your program is up and running, and this is how game creation begins. In reality it's slightly more involved and requires a little more understanding of different types of numbers and how they are used, but nothing that a little experimentation won't easily put to bed. It'll all make even more sense in the next part when we put this into practise and actually begin making things happen on screen. Until then, read through the sprite definition a couple more times and familiarise yourself with the properties and consider how you might modify those properties to make wonderful things happen on screen.

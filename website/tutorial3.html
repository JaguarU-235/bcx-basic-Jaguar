LOLVALAMP project

Extract the ZIP file to your PROJECTS folder and you'll get the "lolvalamp" project. In this micro tutorial, we're simply going to mix and match video modes, object types and "stuff". There's really nothing more to it, but you might just find the tiny amount of "code" to be a bit of an eye-opener.

We're using the "lolvalamp" screen from the Cloudy With a Chance of Metaballs rB+ demo in order to highlight the kind of fun you can have when doing things that maybe you're not meant to. There isn't really any code required to have the Jaguar produce the lolvalamp effect, it's all Raptor and the OP doing their thing - the only real work on our end is creating suitable assets (the blobs in this case) and setting up the video mode and object definitions a certain way, then letting raptor take things from there.

Here is an example of one of the blobs:



It's a 16-bit RGB (RBG for Jaguar), boring, grey, murky 128x128 shaded circle and quite a blurry one at that. The brightest pixel used here is 55,55,55, or #373737 if you're that way inclined. The RGB source gfx were converted to CRY format using ZeroSquare's nodither tool. The object to make use of it had this as part of its declaration:

    dc.l    is_cry      ; (CRY/RGB)                     ; bitmap GFX type
    dc.l    is_trans    ; (TRANSPARENCY)                ; bitmap TRANS flag

Nothing too interesting there. We have our CRY object, but we're not going to use a matching video mode. Where the fun comes in and we get the free metaball-ish effect (not really, but whatevs) is when use an RGB video mode, CRY objects and carefully set a background colour allowing the weird to happen.

Before you first build the project, head to line 21 and change the video mode to "cry_voff" instead of "rgb_voff". Build that and you'll see the kind of thing that was being played around with initially - hoping to have them interact and saturate out to the top end as more piled on, it's a bit meh but it was only an experiment (see the clouds in the Reboot game "Kobayashi Maru" for an example). there's still some fun you could play with there with a few object types, some sort of fake DOF effect for distant lights or something. Anyway, once you're done and have viewed that, change it back to "rgb_voff", build the project once more, and you'll see something more like the CWaCoM screen.

So there's an array of blobs floating around aimlessly, melting into one another as they pass by. There are around 12 or 13 128x128x16bit rmw objects(!), 6 64x64x16bit animated objects and just a couple of the small 32x32x16bit animated objects. Animation of the 128x128s was removed for this demo as it clashed with BG changes (the frames are still there if you wish to reactivate them in rapinit.s, read on for info on BG changes).

You might notice the Object Processor grind a little if you leave it running long enough - nothing here is scripted or planned, it's let loose on rapinit object presets for the purposes of the demonstration. So if you notice tearing when lots of stuff is sat on the same scanline, you've now been blessed with a taste of what an overloaded OP looks like.

What's fun here in this RGB video mode vs the CRY one, is instead of the colours saturating at the high end, they look to clip and appear to loop back around, and that effect looks sort of psychedelic and interesting. The other interesting thing is the objects appear to melt into each other in their "halo" regions as long as the background colour allows for it. There's also the result of brighter spots looping again and creating a kind of topography effect, all quite fun and "free" in hardware.

You'll notice in the demo that the background colour changes from bright yellow to deep red and back over time, this is merely to help demonstrate how inter-object interactivity alters within the larger range. When the background is bright yellow, you'll see the objects saturate out to deep red at the edges and brighter orange in the centre. When the backdrop is deep red, the objects appear as small red blobs with huge yellow halos. At both extremes, the range of the halo region inter-object melting is reduced and can produce some odd artifacting, but it can also offer some weirder-in-a-good-way results - try using some static values instead of allowing cola to be used to alter the value every 5 frames. You could also try altering the BG in a more sensible way than just adding and subtracting 1 to it as that's just daft when you think what you're actually doing outside the safety of the range of greens you are affecting, lol.

Now try changing line 38 to have a larger range of values - 254:

    IF cola=59646 OR cola=59392 THEN

Some more odd stuff occurs out in the further reaches where everything saturates out to yellow as more blue is added, it appears as a kind of mixture of clipping then saturating... someone good with numbers could explain that and make some better use of it, but quite what use this kind of stuff could be beyond demo-ish screens is any body's guess. But regardless, have a play around, pile them high and move your objects in more regular and scripted ways, the results can be really fun! Try all kinds of source graphics with different ranges of shading and mix them in with others, see how they interact. If you want to get really trip-o-tronic, switch varmod on (change vmode to cry_von) and add the 128x128 animation back in (set maxframe on each to 5 in rapinit). Play around with that range of background values... there's likely much more potential for demo-ish/game effects using these properties of the Jaguar.
